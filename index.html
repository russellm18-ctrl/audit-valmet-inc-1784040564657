// Findings

## Atmospheric Reality Gap

Your four hero images do not agree on what Valmet is. Frame one delivers a credible industrial signal — two technicians in a working shop floor, real overhead lighting, authentic environment. That frame earns trust. Then frame two replaces reality with a 3D CAD render and a wireframe schematic on a flat blue field — computer-generated assets that communicate "we couldn't get access to the actual machine." Frame three is the expensive problem: a darkly lit figure draped in white fabric, shot like a perfume commercial or an arts festival reel. No Valmet logo. No product. No operational context. A procurement engineer comparing valve and flow-control vendors on the same afternoon they visit your site will not understand what that image is selling — and will not wait to find out. Frame four returns to a tight macro shot of a machined flange, branded correctly with the Valmet mark. That recovery comes too late. The emotional whiplash between frame three's theatrical darkness and every other frame signals that no single creative director owns this visual system. The trust cost: buyers evaluating six-figure capital equipment contracts read visual inconsistency as operational inconsistency.

## Visual Narrative Coherence

The four frames you're leading with represent four separate visual languages — documentary photography, CGI product rendering, abstract editorial, and branded close-up macro — none of which share lighting temperature, color grade, or compositional logic. That fragmentation tells a high-value buyer that your visual assets were sourced piecemeal, likely from stock libraries, different vendors, and internal render teams, then assembled without a unifying brief. A buyer who arrived expecting the precision of a global industrial automation supplier instead navigates a mood board that changes genre every 400 pixels. The link audit returned 0 broken links across 25 homepage URLs, which is clean infrastructure — but clean links cannot compensate for a hero sequence that fractures brand coherence before a single product page loads. The fracture happens at first impression, which is the only impression that controls whether a buyer clicks deeper or exits to a competitor whose visual story holds together.

## Metadata and Conversion Gaps

Eleven of your 18 homepage content images are missing alt text — a 61% gap. Alt text is not an accessibility checkbox; it is the text layer that search engines index when they cannot see pixels. Every untagged product image, technician photo, and component close-up is invisible to Google Image Search and contributes nothing to your rankings for the technical queries your buyers use. Your page title runs 82 characters, beyond the ~60-character display limit, meaning Google truncates your brand positioning before a searcher reads it. Your meta description is 501 characters — more than double the ~155-character threshold that search engines will render — so the value proposition you wrote for that field is being cut in half in every result snippet. You hold the #1 organic position for "Valmet, Inc," which means branded search is not the problem. The problem is that buyers who land from product-category or application-specific queries hit a homepage whose image infrastructure cannot reinforce the ranking signals that brought them there, and whose hero sequence gives them an immediate reason to question whether they've found the right company.
